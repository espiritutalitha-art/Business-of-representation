Module 3

Feminist Intersectionality

Not all women's experiences of oppression are the same. Race, sexuality, disability, and body size compound and complicate gendered bias. This week's data proves it: fat Latina, LGBTQ+, and disabled fat women are nearly erased entirely.

Module 4

Disability Models

The medical model frames difference as a personal problem to be fixed. Fatphobia has been medicalized in exactly this way — given scientific cover that makes bias feel like fact. Weight gain is coded as moral failure, just as disability once was.

Module 5

Body Size as Structure

Fatphobia is not personal aesthetics or market preference. It is a social justice issue — structural, intersectional, and deeply medicalized — operating through the same systems we've been analyzing all course.

Chapter 4 — Three things to carry in

⚕️

Fatphobia is medicalized

Defined as a medical condition to be diagnosed and treated, fatphobia gains scientific cover that makes bias feel like objective fact — the same logic as the medical model of disability. This is why it remains socially acceptable in ways other forms of bias are not.

Heldman & Bargiachi, 2025, p. 190

🏛️

Fatphobia emerged with racism

The thin beauty standard for white women emerged during the 1700s expansion of slavery. Thinness became a marker of whiteness and racial superiority over Black women of size — a history that makes intersectional analysis essential, not optional.

Heldman & Bargiachi, 2025, p. 196

💼

Fatphobia is a business decision

Who gets cast, how they are costumed, how much screen time they receive — these are production choices. And those choices give "cultural permission" for real-world discrimination in employment, medicine, and everyday life.

Heldman & Bargiachi, 2025, p. 192

The "unfinished thing" — narrative deferral

The industry assigns fat characters specific narrative functions — roles that exist to serve the thin protagonist's story, not to inhabit their own. Chapter 4 identifies the key tropes:

😂

The Comic Relief

Fat women are twice as likely to be portrayed as funny (52.4% vs. 26.5%). Physical shortcomings played for laughs reinforce the idea that people of size are clumsy and incapable.

🧍

The Sidekick

Present for laughs and to advance the protagonist's arc. We are never asked to care about their lives. Their narrative is permanently subordinated to someone thinner.

🤱

The Mamma Hen

Nurturing, supportive, emotionally available — for everyone else. Her own desire, ambition, and story remain permanently offscreen.

Intersectionality in the data

Not all fat women are equally erased — intersecting identities compound the pattern. Some groups are better represented; others nearly disappear entirely.

Well-represented

Underrepresented

What good representation looks like

Positive representations have begun to emerge — and they show us what's possible when fatness is central to a character's story, not the problem she has to overcome.

📺

Shrill — Annie

Fashionable, romantically active, confident — while facing anti-fat bias in her workplace, medical offices, and everyday life. The series intentionally challenges fatphobia: Annie pushes back against body policing, shows joy in eating, and a fat pool party celebrates women of size.

Heldman & Bargiachi, 2025, p. 225

Two models of positive representation

Fatness can be central to a character's story — foregrounded and challenged — or it can be incidental, simply part of who a character is without being their defining feature. Both normalize the idea that the stories of people of size are worth telling.

A note on language

Use: fat

"Fat" is the preferred term in the size acceptance and fat activist movements — a reclaimed, neutral descriptor of body size. Chapter 4 uses it intentionally, and you are encouraged to do the same in your posts.

Heldman & Bargiachi, 2025, p. 189

Avoid unless quoting directly

Stigmatizing clinical language like "overweight" or "obese" medicalizes body size and reinforces the very bias the readings ask you to analyze. Avoid these terms in your own voice.

✍🏽

Language is an analytical choice. How you name something shapes how you are able to think about it.
