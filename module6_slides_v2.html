Unlike other prejudices rooted in fear of difference, ageism uniquely targets our future selves. It is a bias that affects everyone, eventually.

Four Myths of Aging in Media

Myth 1

Older adults end up in nursing homes. In reality, 97% will never require assisted living or nursing home care.

Myth 2

Older adults are inflexible & old-fashioned. Political attitudes remain remarkably stable across a lifetime.

Myth 3

Older adults are bad with technology. 70% purchased new tech last year — but tech is often not designed for them.

Myth 4

Older adults have less or no interest in sex. 2 in 3 adults 65+ are interested in sex; more than half say it's important to quality of life.

Falling Short Across the Board

Intersectional Gaps: Who Is Most Erased?

All data below refers specifically to older adult characters (50+) who also belong to each group — from The Media Reader, Chapter 5

Quality: What Roles Do They Play?

  • 59.9% of older characters are depicted with a negative stereotype — sick, feeble, senile, or a burden to society
  • 1 in 3 older characters shown as stubborn (32.8%) or cranky (31.9%)
  • Ageist language is casual and common: "girls," "young lady," "senior moment"
  • Older women = Sexless Spinster trope; older men = Dirty Old Man trope
  • The Ageless Test: Does a film include at least one woman 50+ who humanly matters to the plot? Only 25% of top films pass.

Chonody et al.: What the Research Tells Us

  • Positive stereotypes dominated (90% contributing to society) — but "successful aging" can also erase diversity and set unrealistic standards
  • Gendered patterns: men → "grumpy old man" / "dirty old man"; women → worried about looks (nearly 50% of female characters)
  • No Black or queer women leads found across the entire 20-year study period
  • Ageism & sexism travel together — films with more ageist content also had more sexist content
"Who is not featured is as important as who is."
