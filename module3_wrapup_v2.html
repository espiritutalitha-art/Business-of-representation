Representation is uneven

Counting women is not enough — we also have to ask which women are visible, how they are framed, and who remains largely absent.

Women of Color

BIPOC women characters have doubled over the past decade — from 23.8% in 2012 to 55.1% in 2022 — yet they remain underrepresented in industry power and are still stereotyped in key ways.

Heldman & Bargiachi, 2025, pp. 120–124

Older Women

One-in-three U.S. women is 50 or older, yet none of the top-grossing films of the past decade feature a woman 50+ lead, and only one-in-four films pass the Ageless Test.

Heldman & Bargiachi, 2025, pp. 134–140

Disabled & Fat Women

Despite one-in-four U.S. women having a disability, disabled women peak at 3.2% of women characters. Only 6.3% of TV shows have a fat woman lead, despite 42.5% of U.S. women being fat.

Heldman & Bargiachi, 2025, pp. 129–143

Barbie as a milestone

Historic achievement

Barbie (2023) is the highest-grossing film ever directed by a woman solo (Greta Gerwig), with a $145M budget — far above the sub-$20M ceiling faced by nearly all women directors (Heldman & Bargiachi, 2025, p. 73). As Lily noted, this makes it an outlier, not a turning point.

Popular feminism

The film embeds feminist language and symbols in mainstream culture — Gerwig described it as feminist "in the way it includes everyone." This is popular feminism at work: accessible, widely circulated, but also susceptible to co-optation (Byrnes, Lorec, & May, 2025).

The polysemous doll

Barbie's ambivalent cultural history — narrow beauty standards alongside diverse careers and identities — makes her polysemous: she can be mainstream, contradictory, empowering, and commodified at once (Byrnes, Lorec, & May, 2025, p. 762).

What the film does boldly

Ken's arc exposes restrictive masculinity. Barbie Land stages a feminist utopia. The "Hi Barbie" scene activates a collective feminine identification that invites spectators in (Byrnes, Lorec, & May, 2025, p. 761). These are genuine provocations worth weighing before naming the film's limits.

"She's Everything": Key Scenes & Themes

🏖️

"Hi Barbie" — Collective Identification

The scene activates a shared feminine community and invites spectators to position both self and other equally as Barbie — a powerful, inclusive gesture (Byrnes, Lorec, & May, 2025, p. 761).

🪄

Galatea's Arc — Becoming Human

Stereotypical Barbie moves from idealized icon to person with agency. Just as she cannot be everything, the film cannot be the final word on feminism — and that is part of its honesty (Byrnes, Lorec, & May, 2025, p. 762).

💼

Ken's Arc — Restrictive Masculinity

Ken's journey into patriarchy and back exposes how "acting like a man" means suppressing vulnerability — millions of boys and men learn early that weakness must stay hidden (Heldman & Bargiachi, 2025, pp. 109–113).

🗣️

The Gloria Monologue

As Abbie observed, the monologue speaks from a white woman's perspective — even as it is delivered by a woman of color. The emotional universalism lands differently depending on whose experience you bring to the theater.

👑

President Barbie — Utopia's Limit

A Black woman holds the highest position in Barbie Land — yet the film never asks why she can only be President in a fictional utopia. Barbie Land treats itself as color-blind, which, as Lily argues, is where universalism becomes exclusion.

The intersectionality critique

White feminism vs. intersectional feminism

White feminism centers privileged women and treats misogyny as universal, affecting all women equally. Intersectional feminism acknowledges how race, class, disability, and sexuality shape different experiences of oppression (Okunoren, 2024).

Barbie Land as inverted patriarchy

Okunoren argues that Barbie Land is not a matriarchy — it is simply an inverted patriarchy. A reversal of a broken system "fails to imagine anything radically different" (Okunoren, 2024).

Lily's "bad poetry" frame

Universalizing language, in trying to speak to everyone, ends up speaking specifically to no one. Stereotypical Barbie cannot represent all Barbies — the plot itself gives her a categorically different experience (cellulite, flat feet, thoughts of death).

🔍

"How can the sole voice and story of a skinny, cis-gendered, able-bodied, white woman empower everybody else?"

Okunoren, 2024

A productive counterpoint

Cam offers an important check on the critique — forcing intersectionality can sometimes weaken the focus of a story that has its own specific history to reckon with.

On the doll's history

Barbie has a US-centric, historically white history — and the film doesn't pretend otherwise. Cam argues there is value in a story that grapples honestly with its own baggage rather than papering over it.

On Barbie Land's internal logic

As a fictional world, Barbie Land operates by different rules. President Barbie, a diverse family structure, and Barbies of all shapes and sizes all exist within it — details the film doesn't erase.

Awards culture reveals the real bias

The Barbieheimer mockery and the Critics' Choice Award going to the hypermasculine "I'm Just Ken" — a joke song — reveal where the industry's sympathies actually lie, regardless of Barbie's box office.

The "both/and" tension

The film questions the system it never fully escapes. That tension is not a flaw to resolve — it is a site for analysis, and it is exactly what a strong critical analysis paper will navigate.

From question to executive challenge

Good industry questions become powerful when they demand a specific, measurable commitment — not just a direction, but a mechanism. Here are the kinds of concrete asks your questions point toward:

💰

Development Fund Tied to Barbie's Profits

A dedicated fund — seeded by Barbie's $1.4B gross — for intersectional women-led projects, particularly from Black, Latine, disabled, and queer women directors who face the sub-$20M budget ceiling.

📊

Screen-Time Equity Benchmark

A studio-level commitment to close the 70/30 screen-time gap within five years, with public reporting — moving from a structural audit to a contractual standard.

🎬

Green-Lighting Policy for Intersectional Leads

Green-lighting criteria that require intersectional lead characters — not ensemble diversity — so that representation moves from background to center, with a specific story to carry.

🤝

Community Partnership & Story Authenticity

Require community partnership consultations during development — not just casting — so that diverse narratives are built with, not for, the communities they represent.

🎀

The data

Structural inequality persists. Systemic change requires fixing hiring and promotion — not celebrating one blockbuster.

The film

Barbie is both a genuine milestone and a film constrained by its own white feminist lens — a "both/and" that rewards analysis.
