Choosing your artifact

You need an artifact where language about body size is doing explicit ideological work — where word choices are deliberate, certain terms appear and others are absent, and those choices have real consequences.

Strong artifact choices
  • A streaming series with a fat lead + press tour language
  • A reality show that medicalizes fat bodies in promos
  • An ad campaign claiming "real" bodies without naming them
  • Studio press materials — interviews, casting announcements, marketing copy

The best artifacts are ones where there's a gap between what the language claims and what the production actually delivers. That gap is where your analysis lives.

Our worked example

The Barbie (2023) press & marketing campaign — a text you already know analytically from Module 3, now examined for what its language about body size is doing ideologically.

Move 1 — Map the language

What terms does the Barbie press campaign use? What's conspicuously absent?

Terms that appear in official materials
curvy diverse real inclusive "Barbie energy"

"Barbie energy" — Vogue, May 2023 — defined as "a certain ineffable combination of beauty and exuberance." Codes thinness as a quality, not a body type. Naturalizes the thin ideal without naming it.

Vogue, May 2023; Mattel/Warner Bros. press materials

Terms conspicuously absent
fat plus-size body size fatphobia

The word "fat" appears almost nowhere in official press materials — despite the campaign explicitly claiming body diversity as a brand value.

Media Reader framework

"Fat" = preferred neutral descriptor (reclaimed)

"Curvy/diverse/real" = euphemisms that aestheticize body size without politicizing it

"Overweight/obese" = pathologizing clinical language to avoid

Heldman & Bargiachi, 2025, p. 189

The absence of "fat" in a campaign built around body representation is not neutral. It is a choice. Your analysis needs to name what that choice is doing.

The Amy Schumer moment

The one time the press tour engaged directly with body size — and what the studio's response reveals.

What happened

Amy Schumer was originally cast as Barbie, then departed. In press coverage, she noted: "Plus-size is considered size 16 in America." The implication: the studio's vision of a "diverse" Barbie still defaulted to a thin ideal.

Schumer, quoted in press coverage, 2017

The studio's response

Silence. No press statement. No reframing. "Barbie energy" language continued unchallenged — and Margot Robbie was cast.

This is Chapter 4's argument in action: anti-fat bias is socially acceptable precisely because it goes unquestioned.

Heldman & Bargiachi, 2025, p. 190

🔍

The analytical move here

The campaign could simultaneously claim to celebrate "real" and "diverse" bodies while defaulting to the thin ideal as its unmarked norm — and face almost no mainstream press scrutiny for the contradiction.

That contradiction — between the language of inclusion and the production decision — is your argument.

What "diverse" actually means

When studios use "curvy" and "diverse" rather than "fat," they perform inclusion without committing to it — capturing the ticket purchases and cultural goodwill of 42.5% of American women while keeping the film anchored to the thin ideal.

That's not just a language problem. That's a revenue strategy.

The Rep Project, 2022

Your claim

Language manages the optics of inclusion without changing the underlying production logic. Executives can point to the word "diverse" in press materials — the Rep Project data shows what "diverse" actually means on screen.

What does this language do in the broader economy of representation?

💡 This is your analytical core — the bridge between words and business logic. Make a clear, arguable claim. Don't just describe the gap; explain who benefits from it.

Move 3 — Micro-guidelines: your questions

I'm not giving you the guidelines — that's your job. But here are the questions that should be driving them. Think about both perspectives — and how they can reinforce each other.

For writers & showrunners

When you write a character description or casting breakdown, which terms are you using to describe body size — and what do those terms signal to casting about which bodies are actually acceptable? How does the Media Reader's distinction between reclaimed and pathologizing language change what you put on the page?

For development executives

The Rep Project shows fat disabled and fat LGBTQ+ women at or near 0% in both film and TV. If a project is being marketed on body diversity, what production-stage mechanism would actually close the gap between that language and those numbers? Think specific — a benchmark, a protocol, a greenlight condition.

The Rep Project, 2022

For marketing & publicity

If your campaign uses words like "diverse," "real," or "inclusive," what specific bodies does that language actually commit you to? And if there's a gap between your marketing language and what's on screen — who notices, and what does it cost you? The Rep Project's 42.5% benchmark is a good place to start building that business case.

For all three

Language can lead — it can signal intent and shape culture. But can it change anything on its own, without the production decisions to back it up? What would it look like for the creative and business sides to move together, rather than one getting ahead of the other?
