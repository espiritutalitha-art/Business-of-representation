Key Framework · Olkin (2022)

Three Models of Disability

🕯 Moral Model

Disability tied to character, karma, or spiritual meaning. On screen: evil villains with physical differences, or "chosen" survivors with miraculous strength. Disability as sign.

🏥 Medical Model

Disability as pathology to be corrected toward "normalcy." On screen: the hopeless disabled person saved by an able-bodied friend, or the hero who overcomes their condition. Disability as problem.

🌐 Social Model

Disability as identity shaped by environment and social barriers — not the individual. On screen: multiple disabled characters forming community, advocating for change. Disability as context.

The model an industry operates from shapes every casting decision, every script choice, every role it offers — or withholds.

Key Data · Conroy et al. (2024)

The Representation Gap

📺 TV Representation

Characters with disabilities ranged from 2.6% (2020) to 4.7% (2021) — against a 28.7% population benchmark. Progress is real but fragile.

🎬 Authentic Casting

Only 21% of disabled characters are portrayed by actors with the same or similar disability. 79% of disabled roles still go to non-disabled actors.

📡 Platform Gap

Streaming: 4.7% vs. Broadcast/Cable: 3.3%. The difference matters — but it doesn't mean the medical model has loosened its grip on either platform.

🏆 Awards Gap

No Emmy for Best Comedy has ever gone to a show with a disabled lead in 74 years. Only 5% of Best Drama Emmys feature disabled leads.

Key Framework · GDI Creator Questions

How Are Disabled Characters Portrayed?

Common Tropes to Resist

  • The Bitter Crip — tragic, depressed, isolated
  • The Super Crip — 22.9% of disabled characters; transcendence as the only story
  • Inspiration Porn — disability as motivation for non-disabled characters
  • The Cure Narrative — disability resolved, "normalcy" restored
  • The Brave Overcomer — language like "fighter," "warrior," "survivor" frames disability as the enemy

The GDI Creator Questions

  • Multidimensional? Traits, humor, flaws, and goals beyond disability
  • Subtle language? Does framing reinforce tragedy or inspiration tropes — even unintentionally?
  • Diverse experience? Range across skin tone, body type, age, class, disability type
  • Authentic casting? Actors with lived experience, and disabled professionals behind the camera
Key Data · The Media Reader (Heldman & Bargiachi, 2025)

Disability & Intersectionality

🎨 Race

White characters are 1.5× more likely to be depicted as disabled than characters of color (p. 156). In TV, disabled BIPOC characters do not consistently appear at population parity.

⚧ Gender

71.4% of on-screen disability representation is male vs. 28.6% female — even though women make up half the disabled population. Some years show 0% disabled women leads in top films (p. 171).

🏳️‍🌈 LGBTQIA+

LGBTQIA+ characters appear with disabilities at 8.5% vs. 3.7% for non-LGBTQIA+ — but zero disabled film leads who are LGBTQIA+ in the last decade (p. 180).

The question isn't just: is disability represented? It's: whose disability is represented — and whose remains invisible?

Pushing the Analysis Further

❓ Why does streaming (4.7%) show higher disability representation than broadcast (3.3%)?

This difference isn't evidence that streaming has rejected the medical model — it's evidence that streaming can better afford to accommodate it. Broadcast operates on tighter schedules, live production demands, and advertiser pressure to maximize mainstream appeal. These conditions apply medical model logic most aggressively: disabled actors are read as production risks. Streaming's longer timelines and prestige-driven, subscriber-based model slightly lower the perceived "cost" of disability. The medical model's logic remains intact on both platforms. Streaming just has more resources to manage it.

❓ Whose disability experience is still missing — even when representation improves?

Even when representation numbers inch upward, the gains are not distributed equally. White disabled characters are 1.5× more likely to be depicted than characters of color. Disabled BIPOC characters don't consistently appear at population parity on TV. There have been zero disabled LGBTQIA+ film leads in a decade. A film like Out of My Mind can be genuinely groundbreaking — and still center a white, young, female experience of CP while leaving other disabled BIPOC and LGBTQIA+ experiences invisible. Progress that only represents some disabled people is partial progress.

Key Takeaway · Saba (2025) + Easterseals

What Authentic Casting Actually Requires

🎭 The Pool Gap Problem

The primary barrier to authentic casting is the "pool gap" — disabled actors are often not registered with traditional agencies. Passive casting (waiting for submissions) guarantees failure. Authentic casting requires active outreach: medical community pipelines, NGO partnerships, grassroots referral networks.

🎥 360-Degree Representation

True representation doesn't end at the lens. Disabled professionals behind the camera — in writing rooms, directing chairs, production roles — are essential. The Easterseals Disability Film Challenge models this: building a specialized talent pipeline that reduces future sourcing costs and elevates narrative accuracy.

📝 Narrative ROI

Authentic attachment prevents costly medical inaccuracies and "cringe" reshoots. When Phoebe-Rae Taylor brought her lived CP experience to Out of My Mind, the production gained on-set problem-solving that consultants alone couldn't provide. Lived experience is a creative asset, not a liability.

🌟 Gavin McHugh & 9-1-1

McHugh's forearm crutches weren't written around — they were written into the show. The skateboarding sequence in Season 3 shows Christopher's disability driving emotional truth and narrative conflict. This is what "disability as identity, not obstacle" looks like in practice.
