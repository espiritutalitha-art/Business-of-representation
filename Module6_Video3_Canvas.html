6.3 Tips for Your Organizer

Four Keys to a Strong Synthesis

🎯
Choose an identity you found genuinely compelling. The more invested you are, the stronger your synthesis will be.
🔗
Don't just summarize — connect. Ask yourself: what do these two sources say together that neither says alone?
📊
Be specific with your data. Don't write "older women are underrepresented." Write "older women made up only 25% of older film characters in 2022, far below the 51% baseline."
🏷️
Use the Chonody tropes precisely. Nostalgia, rigidity, worried about looks, grumpy old man, dirty old man, successful aging — these are powerful analytical categories.

By The Media Reader's standards, Grace and Frankie checks real boxes: two older women leads, long run, high visibility in premium streaming space.
But here's the first question the quantitative lens can't fully answer: both Grace and Frankie are white, upper middle class, and heterosexual (until the show's central plot disruption). The Media Reader's baselines count presence — but not always whose presence, or on whose terms.
6.4 Example Grace and Frankie — Chonody's Framework

Running It Through Three Stereotype Categories

✅ Successful Aging
Both leads are active, independent, and contributing — Grace runs a business, Frankie is an artist and activist. Chonody codes these as positive portrayals. But the "successful aging" model can be exclusionary — it sets idealized standards that erase illness, precarity, and dependence.
⚠️ Worried About Looks
Grace's arc is explicitly shaped by anxiety about appearance and aging — she founded a beauty company. Chonody would flag this: nearly half of female characters in the study were worried about their looks. Grace fits squarely in this category.
⚠️ Rigidity
Frankie resists this trope — open, adaptive, unconventional. But Grace spends much of the early series clinging to status and appearance. Chonody would code this as rigidity toward aging/life — a stereotype that's eventually complicated, but very much present.
6.4 Example The Tension + Industry Implications

What Each Methodology Makes Visible

📊 Quantitative Lens Sees:

Presence and counts it as progress. Two older women leads, 7 seasons, high visibility. Easier to sell in a boardroom.

🔬 Qualitative Lens Asks:

Presence on what terms? Both leads white, upper-class, heterosexual. Worried about looks. That question changes everything.

Studio executive take: Quantitative data justifies the investment — the silver economy, demographics, audience reach. Qualitative analysis stress-tests the storytelling and protects against backlash. The studios getting it right use both.
Risk: Quant only → Miss the opportunity to tell stories about older women who aren't white, wealthy, and conventionally beautiful
Risk: Qual only → Can critique everything but struggle to make the business case that gets the project greenlit
6.4 Example Creative Recommendation

One Evidence-Based Narrative Change

"Introduce a recurring older woman of color — not as a supporting character but as a peer with her own storyline, her own business stakes, her own complicated relationship to aging."

📖
Grounded in the data: Older BIPOC women are nearly entirely absent from television leads (Media Reader Ch. 5). That absence is a documented gap — and a storytelling opportunity.
✍️
Your recommendation should be: Specific (not just "be more diverse"), grounded in evidence from both readings, and actionable — what would it look like, and why does it matter?
"Who is not featured is as important as who is." — Chonody et al.
