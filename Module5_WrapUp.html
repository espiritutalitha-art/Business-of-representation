How Hollywood turns body size into a business decision — and what your work this module revealed about the mechanisms behind it.

Core Frameworks

What We Were Asking

Fatphobia as Industry Logic

Anti-fat bias isn't only a cultural attitude — it's built into casting decisions, narrative conventions, and marketing assumptions that shape what gets made.

The "Unfinished Thing"

Lindy West: fat characters are treated as incomplete versions of themselves — their full personhood deferred until the body changes or the arc resolves.

Cultural Permission

Media representations don't just reflect discrimination — they license it, giving audiences permission to treat fat people as less credible, less desirable, less worthy.

Gatekeeping & Value

The industry decides which bodies are bankable. Fat bodies are overwhelmingly cast as comic, cautionary, or conditional — rarely as simply central.

Discussion 5.2 · Student Work

Abbie on Superbad

Abbie Cramer

Abbie chose Seth — the film's central protagonist — and argued that even leads aren't exempt from the "unfinished thing" logic. Seth's body is framed as the origin of his dysfunction, his desperation, and his cruelty — not just a detail of his character.

"Seth might be better understood through the lens of conditional personhood: his full humanity is always just out of reach, promised once the insecurity his body caused is resolved."

— Analysis of Seth Cohen, Superbad (2007)

What this complicates: The "unfinished thing" isn't only for sidekicks. A fat protagonist can still be subject to the same deferral logic — and that's a more insidious form of it.

Discussion 5.2 · Student Work

Lily on Sierra Burgess Is a Loser

Lily

Lily's analysis surfaced a key tension: Sierra Burgess presents itself as empowering, but its visual and narrative logic pathologizes Sierra's body at every level — from the fragmenting mirror scene to the framing of her cruelty as a psychological product of being fat.

"The film doesn't show a fatphobic society mistreating Sierra. It shows a girl whose body has apparently made her cruel and broken — and that lets the audience off the hook entirely."

— On Sierra Burgess Is a Loser (Netflix, 2018)

What this adds: Sympathetic representation can still do ideological damage. The most insidious fatphobia doesn't mock — it explains.

Discussion 5.2 · Response Posts

Where the Conversation Got Interesting

Lily → Cam

Lily brought in a Biggest Loser contestant's own words — "if I lost the weight, loving someone will be so much easier" — as living proof that media tropes don't stay on screen. She then identified a sharp contradiction: a show built entirely around fat bodies generating $96.6M annually for a network that otherwise marginalizes them.

The industry profits from fat bodies as spectacle while refusing to treat them as narrative subjects.

Lily → Abbie

Lily introduced Paul Blart: Mall Cop — a fat protagonist who is also intelligent, desirable, and a good father — and asked whether Kevin James co-writing his own vehicle changes the representational calculus.

Can a fat creator reclaim a trope from the inside — or does the market still set the terms?

Synthesis

What Your Examples Revealed Together

  • Fat protagonists are not exempt from deferral logic — centrality is not the same as full personhood (Seth, Superbad)
  • Sympathetic framing can reproduce the same ideological damage as mockery — by locating the problem in the fat person rather than in the fatphobic world (Sierra Burgess)
  • Media tropes become real belief systems — the Biggest Loser contestant's words show the direct line between screen and self-perception
  • The industry's relationship to fat bodies is contradictory: lucrative as spectacle, marginal as subjects
  • Creator identity complicates but doesn't resolve these patterns — the market still structures what fat creators can make (Paul Blart)

Peer Review · What Strong Feedback Does

From Diagnosis to Direction

Both peer reviews this module showed genuine engagement and accurate identification of gaps. The growth edge for everyone — in peer review as in paper writing — is the same move:

Developing

"Your theoretical framework needs more data and your argument feels vague."

Exceptional

"In paragraph 3, your vampire example disconnects from the media economics frame — here is a specific source that could bridge them, and here is what it would do for your argument."

The best peer feedback names a specific moment, proposes a specific direction, and explains why it would strengthen the argument. Diagnosis is the floor. Direction is the goal.

Assignment 5.4 · Terminology & Ideological Work

Language as a Weapon — and a Choice

Lily · The Biggest Loser

Lily shows that the show pathologizes fat bodies through every language layer simultaneously: the voiceover ("they're fat!"), the elimination line ("cut the fat"), the editing of bodies alongside donuts, and the public weigh-in ritual. No neutral or affirming terms appear at all — "people of size," "plus-size," and "fat" as a neutral descriptor are entirely absent.

Key insight: The show doesn't just reflect stigma — it is structurally designed around humiliation, making positive language about fat bodies formally impossible.

Cam · The DUFF

Cam shows that The DUFF occupies more ambiguous ideological ground: it avoids medicalized terms like "obese" or "overweight," but still frames "fat" as a social insult Bianca must overcome. The film critiques the label while depending on the stigma attached to it.

Key insight: A film can avoid pathologizing language and still do ideological damage — by treating fatness as a social problem the character must resolve rather than a neutral aspect of identity.

Together, these two examples reveal the full spectrum: from a show that weaponizes body-size language overtly and structurally (The Biggest Loser) to a film that reproduces stigma more quietly, through what it assumes its audience already believes (The DUFF). Language does ideological work even when it is trying not to.
