Reading the Numbers

GLAAD SRI Guided Reading & Assignment 8.3

Why This Reading Matters

A live industry document — not just a reading.

  • Tracks current, measurable behavior across 10 major studios and 250 films.
  • Asks not just how many LGBTQ+ characters exist, but what kind — and with how much narrative weight.
  • A studio can improve a number and still not tell a better story.
  • A character can appear on screen and still not represent meaningful inclusion.
  • That distinction is exactly what this course is built to help you make.

Four Things to Notice as You Read

These are the lenses that connect the SRI to your assignments.

01

Screen time matters. A character who appears for 30 seconds is not the same as one who drives the plot.

02

Identity categories matter. GLAAD tracks gender identity, race, sexuality, disability, and HIV status — not just "queer or not."

03

Quantity ≠ quality. More characters does not mean better representation. The SRI makes this point repeatedly.

04

The report makes recommendations. GLAAD doesn't just diagnose — it tells studios what to do. That is the model for 8.3.

The Vito Russo Test

A baseline — not a finish line.

Named after GLAAD co-founder and Celluloid Closet author Vito Russo. Inspired by the Bechdel Test.

Three criteria to pass

Identifiably LGBTQ+The character's identity is clearly established on screen.

More than a labelNot predominantly defined by their sexual orientation or gender identity.

Tied to the plotRemoving them would have a significant effect on the story.

Passing the test does not mean the portrayal is great — it means it clears a minimum threshold. Films like Zoolander 2 and The Gentlemen passed and still contained offensive content.

Studio Profiles

A24 vs. Sony: Intent vs. Inertia

A24 — "Good" ✦

  • First major studio with over half its slate LGBTQ+-inclusive (56%, 9 of 16 films)
  • Queer characters lead their own stories across genres — never sanitized
  • 8 of 9 inclusive films passed the Vito Russo Test
  • Standouts: Love Lies Bleeding, Problemista, Queer, I Saw the TV Glow
  • GLAAD note: still needs more trans stories and LGBTQ+ characters of color

Sony — "Insufficient"

  • Only 4 of 33 films (12%) were LGBTQ+-inclusive — a year-over-year drop
  • Zero LGBTQ+ leads across the entire slate
  • 3 of 4 inclusive films passed the Vito Russo Test — but barely
  • In Tarot, queer characters were killed off while the straight couple survived
  • Key lesson: passing baseline tests ≠ meaningful representation
Netflix · "Poor" Rating

Visibility is not the same as responsible representation.

  • Stereotypical depiction: GLAAD argues Emilia Pérez relies on harmful, outdated tropes rather than authentic lived experiences.
  • Trivializing gender-affirming care: The film opens with a musical number about surgeries GLAAD describes as wildly inaccurate and deeply insulting.
  • The "deceptive" plot: A Mrs. Doubtfire-style trope — faking death to transition — reinforces dangerous myths weaponized against trans people.
  • The executive education gap: Netflix had already released Disclosure, warning against exactly these harms — then mounted a multimillion-dollar awards campaign for Emilia Pérez.

A character can be visible and still reproduce harm. That is the lesson for Section 2 of 8.3.

Observations & Recommendations

What the SRI wants the industry to do.

  • Lead narrative development — Move beyond background cameos to characters who drive the plot.
  • Authentic trans casting — No cisgender actors in trans roles; collaborate with trans writers from the start.
  • Racial diversity — Films with LGBTQ+ characters of color grossed $1.2B globally in 2024. Not a niche market.
  • Gender parity — LGBTQ+ women hit 50% of all characters for only the second time ever. Make it permanent.
  • Bisexual+ representation — Bi+ individuals make up 57% of the real-world LGBTQ+ community. They represent 10% of tracked characters.
  • HIV narratives — Zero HIV characters for the second consecutive year. Zero is not neutral.

Assignment 8.3

Character Case Study:
Beyond the Checkbox

Apply The Media Reader Chapter 6 + GLAAD's SRI to one LGBTQ+ character. Move from critique to standards — from analysis to professional recommendation.

Choose one

Sophia — OITNB
Nick — Heartstopper
Brandon — Boys Don't Cry

Format

500–600 words (written)
or 5–7 min (audio)
Due: Sun Jul 13

Audience

Write to a colleague
in a creative or
development context.

Section 1

Context and Identity

  • Briefly establish who the character is.
  • Identify intersecting identities — race, gender identity, sexuality, class, age, anything that matters for this portrayal.
  • Name the stereotypes already in the air from Chapter 6: the tragic queer, the predatory gay, the deceptive or criminalized trans woman.
  • Consider the setting and genre. A prison, a teen romance, or a tragedy-based narrative each carries representational risks before the story even begins.

This section sets up everything that follows. Precise framing here makes Sections 2–4 much stronger.

Rubric note: "Analysis connects theory to text, drawing on specific textual evidence."
Section 2

Harm vs. Complexity

What the character contests

  • Identify two specific ways the character resists or complicates a harmful stereotype
  • e.g., Sophia as a fully realized parent and worker
  • e.g., Nick as a bisexual teen whose story centers joy, not trauma
  • e.g., Brandon exposing the violence of transphobia rather than spectacularizing it

Where it still falls short

  • Identify at least one risk or limitation — even in a progressive portrayal
  • e.g., the carceral setting framing Sophia's story
  • e.g., centering white or cis-appearing protagonists
  • e.g., using real-life tragedy as narrative engine

The rubric rewards genuinely balanced analysis — neither uncritical praise nor dismissal.

Section 3

The GLAAD Lens

  • Would this film or series help or hurt a studio's SRI score?
  • Would it pass the Vito Russo Test? Walk through all three criteria.
  • Does your GLAAD answer match your Section 2 analysis?

Sometimes the answers line up. Sometimes they don't. That gap is not a problem — it is the point.

Rubric (Exceptional): "Identifies a specific and meaningful tension between SRI-oriented assessment and qualitative evaluation, and articulates what that tension reveals about the structural limits of quantitative measurement."
Section 4

Your Industry Recommendation

Think like a professional. You are in a meeting with studio executives. Make a case they can act on.

1

One exemplary element — What would you hold up as a model for other productions?

2

One caution — What should a studio not replicate, and why?

3

One specific change — If this story were greenlit today, what would you recommend? Ground it in Chapter 6 or GLAAD data.

✓

Be concrete. Not "make it better" — say what changes, and why that matters for the story and audience.

What Strong Work Looks Like

Four things the rubric is looking for.

  • Be specific. Name the trope. Name the scene. Name the GLAAD criterion. Vague generalities won't get you far.
  • Be balanced. Show what works and what doesn't. The rubric rewards nuance — not cheerleading, not dismissal.
  • Show the difference between counting and evaluating. That distinction is one of the most important ideas in this module.
  • Write like you're making a case to a colleague. Thoughtful, professional, grounded — not overly academic and not overly casual.

500–600 words is not a lot of space. Every sentence should be doing analytical work. If a sentence is just summarizing the show, cut it and replace it with analysis.
