Peer Review
Guide

How to give feedback that actually helps — and how to use feedback to strengthen your Critical Analysis Paper.

Draft due Thursday · Peer response due Sunday

Why peer review matters

We are the worst readers
of our own work.

The problem
We know what we meant to say — so we read what we intended, not what's actually on the page. A fresh reader sees things the writer genuinely cannot.
The professional parallel
Writers' rooms give notes. Development executives push back on early drafts. Editors ask the questions the writer stopped asking. Peer review is a version of that — and it's a skill you will use throughout your career.
Your goal: Leave your partner with something they can actually use — a sharper argument, a more specific example, a tension they hadn't considered, or a concrete next step for their final draft.

Step 1 · Due Thursday

Post your working draft
(1,000–1,200 words)

1️⃣
Introduction — your hook, media text context, and a clear working thesis
2️⃣
Theoretical Framework & Literature Review — define your two concepts, supported by academic and industry sources
3️⃣
Analysis Outline — bullet points or brief outline of the specific scenes, characters, or data you plan to analyze
⭐
Author's Note (at the top) — 2–3 sentences telling your partner what you're struggling with most, or what specific area you want feedback on. The more specific you are, the more useful the feedback you'll receive.

The Author's Note — your most important two sentences

Be specific about
what you need.

❌ Too vague
"I'd love any feedback on my draft — thanks!"
This invites generic responses and tells your partner nothing about where you need the most help.
✅ Specific and actionable
"I'm not sure whether my thesis is arguable enough — it might be too descriptive. I'm also uncertain whether I'm applying intersectionality correctly in the third paragraph, or whether I'm just naming it without showing it at work."
This tells your partner exactly where to focus, and gives them a concrete question to answer.

Step 2 · How to read your partner's draft

Read like a thoughtful editor,
not a proofreader.

📋
Open the final assignment instructions and rubric in another window while you read — that's the standard you're evaluating against.
🚫
Do not focus on grammar or spelling. Focus on the core argument, the use of theory, and the integration of evidence.
🔁
Read the full draft once without stopping to take notes. Then ask yourself: What is this paper arguing? If you can't answer that in one clear sentence, that is your most important piece of feedback.
The one-sentence test: Can you summarize the paper's main argument in a single sentence? Try it before you begin filling out the feedback form. Your summary will be the first thing you share with your partner.

Step 3 · The Peer Feedback Form

Four sections. Fill out every one.

1
Thesis & Argument — Summarize the thesis in your own words first. Is it specific and arguable? Does it name both the media text and the frameworks?
2
Theoretical Framework & Evidence — Which concept is clearest? Which needs more support? Are sources integrated as evidence, or dropped as decorative checkboxes?
3
The Analysis Outline — Will the planned examples prove the thesis? What counterargument, challenge, or intersectional factor has the author not yet considered?
4
Final Thoughts — Address the Author's Note directly. Give 1–2 specific, actionable next steps for expanding this draft into the final 2,500-word paper.
⚠️ Always address the Author's Note. Your partner told you what they needed — ignoring it is the peer review equivalent of not answering the question.

What strong feedback looks like

Move beyond agreement.
Aim for proposition.

❌ Weak feedback
"Great thesis! I really liked how you applied intersectionality. Your sources seem solid and I think this will be a strong paper."
Tells the writer nothing they can use. Doesn't summarize the argument, identify anything to develop, or address the Author's Note.
✅ Strong feedback
"Your thesis argues that Reservation Dogs challenges the Hollywood Indian stereotype by centering Indigenous creative authority — that's clear and arguable. Your strongest section is the visibility and erasure definition, which is precise and well-sourced. Where you need more work is the intersectionality application — you mention it but the paper doesn't yet show how gender and class intersect with Indigeneity in specific scenes. For your Author's Note question about scope: I'd narrow your research question to one season so your analysis can go deeper rather than wider."
Summarizes the argument, names what's working, identifies a specific gap, and answers the Author's Note directly.
✍️

Your partner is counting
on you.

The best final papers almost always go through a peer review where both people pushed each other harder than they expected. Take it seriously, and you will get something valuable back.
