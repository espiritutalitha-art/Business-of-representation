A guided walkthrough of your major written assignment — due Week 6.

2,500–3,000 words · APA 7th edition · 8–10 sources

What this assignment is

This is an argument,
not a summary.

What you will do
Choose a real media text. Analyze it through two theoretical frameworks. Ground your argument in academic scholarship and current industry data. Build a claim about what your example reveals, challenges, or perpetuates about how media represents identity.
What this is not
Not a review. Not a plot summary. Not a personal reflection. This is the professional version of everything you have been practicing in your discussion posts and written analyses — rigorous, research-based, and argument-driven.

Step 2 · Formulate your research question

Your research question is
the spine of the paper.

🔍
Pattern 1
How does [media text] challenge or reinforce dominant stereotypes about [identity group]?
📊
Pattern 2
In what ways does [media text] reflect broader industry trends in representation and inclusion?
🔬
Pattern 3
How do theoretical frameworks like intersectionality or gender performativity illuminate the complexities of representation in [media text]?
Strong questions point toward an argument, not a summary. They cannot be answered with "yes" or "no." They are specific enough that your reader knows exactly what the paper will do.

Step 3 · Apply theoretical frameworks

Two lenses, applied together,
see more than either alone.

🔀
Intersectionality
How multiple identities intersect in representation
🎭
Gender Performance
How gender is constructed and performed in media
👁
Visibility & Erasure
Who appears, who is absent, and what that signals
💰
Media Economics
How industry structures shape representation
🌍
Social Model
Disability as systemic barrier, not individual deficit
🏁
Race & Ethnicity
How race and power dynamics are depicted or challenged
Key move: Don't just define the frameworks separately — explain why these two, applied together to this text, produce an argument that neither one could produce alone.

Step 4 · Research your sources

8–10 sources across
three categories.

Academic
Peer-reviewed sources
Articles and book chapters on your frameworks, your text, or comparable examples. Start with JSTOR, Communication Abstracts, and Film Quarterly.
Industry Data
Reports & analytics
UCLA Hollywood Diversity Report, GLAAD Studio Responsibility Index, Geena Davis Institute reports, box office and streaming data.
Reception
Critical & popular
Reviews, creator interviews, audience responses, commentary from industry professionals. Evidence that the text landed — or didn't — in the cultural conversation.
Do not over-rely on a single source. The strongest papers use each source type to do different work: academic sources define and ground the frameworks; industry data contextualizes the example; reception sources show how it registered culturally.

Step 5 · Write your analysis

Six sections. One argument.

Introduction
~250 words · Hook, context, research question, frameworks overview, thesis
Background
~400–500 words · Production context, industry trends, situate within broader debates
Frameworks
~400–500 words · Define concepts, explain why these two frameworks for this text
Analysis ★
~1,000–1,200 words · Close reading of specific scenes, characters, campaigns; integrate data and reception; address strengths and limits intersectionally
Discussion
~400–500 words · Broader significance, social/cultural/economic implications, future directions
Conclusion
~150 words · Summarize argument, restate significance of critical analysis
🚀

Start now. The best
papers need time.

This week, identify two or three possible case studies and begin thinking about which theoretical frameworks apply. You don't need to commit yet — but the students who do the best work give themselves room to explore before they narrow in.
