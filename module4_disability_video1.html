🎙 Voiceover Nearly one in three Americans lives with a disability. That's the largest minority group in the country. Yet over seven years of scripted television—from 2016 to 2023—characters with disabilities never broke 4.7% of the total. And of those roles, only 21% were played by disabled actors. We have to ask: who is actually telling these stories—and does it matter?

🎙 Voiceover When non-disabled actors portray disability, it's not just a casting choice—it forecloses an opportunity for a disabled professional to work, build a career, and shape how their community is represented. United Cerebral Palsy argues this gap costs the industry in authenticity, in storytelling quality, and in the trust of the audiences who live this reality every day.

🎙 Voiceover The data gets harder when we ask whose disability the industry finds worth portraying. White characters are one-and-a-half times more likely to be shown with a disability than characters of color. LGBTQIA+ characters appear with disabilities at twice the rate of straight characters—often in ways that conflate queerness with illness. The Media Reader calls this the intersectional lens, and it's the lens you'll use throughout this module.

Your Analytical Toolkit

Three models explain the "invisible logic"
behind every disability portrayal.

⚖️
Moral Model — disability as flaw, punishment, or villainous marker
e.g. disabled villain tropes; "cured" character as reward
🏥
Medical Model — disability as deficit to fix; the individual is the "problem"
e.g. storylines resolved only by cure; inspiration-porn narratives
🌍
Social Model — disability as product of inaccessible environments & systems
e.g. script barriers, set inaccessibility, exclusion from production
🎙 Voiceover Rhoda Olkin at the APA gives us three models of disability—moral, medical, and social. These aren't just academic categories. They are the invisible logic running beneath casting decisions, character arcs, and narrative resolutions. By the end of this module, you'll be able to watch any scene with a disabled character and identify exactly which model is at work.

The Geena Davis Institute · March 2025

Three questions every creator—
and every analyst—should ask.

1️⃣
Are disabled characters multidimensional?
Do they have goals, relationships, and a life beyond their disability?
2️⃣
Does the script's language reinforce tragedy or inspiration tropes?
Does the character inspire pity, awe, or authentic humanity?
3️⃣
Does the portrayal reflect the full diversity of disabled experience?
Across race, class, age, sexuality, and disability type?
🎙 Voiceover The Geena Davis Institute hands creators—and critics—a practical checklist in their 2025 report. These three questions structure your written analysis this week. They connect everything you read to the concrete decisions writers, directors, and producers make every single day on set.
