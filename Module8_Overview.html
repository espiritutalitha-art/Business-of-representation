Reading 1 · The Media Reader, Ch. 6

A Century of Coded, Erased & Villainized

1895–1968: The Hays Code Era

Homosexuality prohibited as "normal." Queer characters appear as villains & punchlines. Rebecca (1941), Rope (1948), Psycho (1960) use queerness to amplify "perversion."

1968–1990: Post-Code, Still Homophobic

The Killing of Sister George (1968) gets an X. Cruising (1980) equates gayness with danger. Homosexuality remains a DSM disorder until 1974.

1991–2010: New Queer Cinema

B. Ruby Rich coins "New Queer Cinema." My Own Private Idaho, Philadelphia, To Wong Foo. A-list actors play queer roles. But tropes persist beneath the surface.

2017–Present: Watershed Moments

Moonlight wins Best Picture (2017). 2019 is a watershed year: Portrait of a Lady on Fire, Booksmart. Yet GLAAD data shows inclusion now trending downward.

Why Representation Matters

This Is a Public Health Issue

41%
of young LGBTQ+ people seriously considered suicide in the past year — twice the rate of non-LGBTQ+ peers
Even higher risk:

Young people of color, transgender, and nonbinary individuals face disproportionately greater risk for death by suicide.

✅ Positive Representation

Studies of LGBTQ+ youth show that seeing queer characters in film and TV validated and legitimized their sexuality and identity.

❌ Harmful Stereotypes

Stereotypical or negative representations — queer characters as punchlines, victims of violence, or comic relief — damage how LGBTQ+ people see themselves.

The Media Reader Ch. 6 · Quality of Representation

The Tropes That Persist

🎭
The Tragic Gay / Crazy Lesbian

Girl, Interrupted, Black Swan, Mulholland Drive. 35.2% of lesbian & bisexual TV characters die vs. 6.2% of gay men.

👀
The Lipstick Lesbian

Sexual objectification of lesbian characters designed for the heterosexual male gaze — visibility without dignity.

🔀
The Confused Bisexual

Bisexuality framed as accidental, unstable, or "a stop on the way to gay." Rarely shown in men — which is why Nick in Heartstopper matters.

⚠️
The Deceptive Trans Woman

54% of trans characters negatively portrayed. 1 in 5 depicted as sex workers. 61% of trans storylines contain anti-trans language.

🌈
The Flamboyant Gay Man

Some progress: Schitt's Creek's David as pansexual, gay men in children's shows. But stereotypes persist — see Euphoria (2019).

✨
Normalization (Progress)

Brooklyn Nine-Nine, Stranger Things, The Mitchells vs. The Machines (first lesbian in kids' film, 2021). Queerness as part of normal life.

Reading 2 · GLAAD Studio Responsibility Index

Your Primary Analytical Tool: The Vito Russo Test

Modeled after the Bechdel Test, GLAAD's benchmark asks three questions about every LGBTQ+ character:

1
Identifiable Identity
The film must feature a character who is identifiably lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, and/or queer.
2
Beyond the Label
The character cannot be solely defined by their sexual orientation or gender identity. They must have the same depth as straight characters.
3
Plot Integration
The character must be inherently tied into the plot. If removed, the story's progression or ending must be significantly impacted.
⚡ The "Under One Minute" Check: Studios are penalized when queer characters appear for less than 60 seconds. In 2025, 37% of all counted LGBTQ+ characters failed this threshold.

The Productive Tension

Two Frameworks — One Question

📊
GLAAD · The Business Case

Frames LGBTQ+ exclusion as a missed business opportunity. Data-driven scorecards. Market logic. Corporate accountability. Inclusion is commercially smart.

📖
The Media Reader · Social Justice

Roots inclusion in representational harm and justice. Tropes as structural violence. Suicide statistics. The community's right to see itself with dignity.

In a moment of escalating anti-LGBTQ+ politics and corporate DEI retreats, understanding how these two framings complement and clash with each other is not just an academic exercise — it is a professional one.

GLAAD's Structural Critique

In times of industry contraction, studios default to "safe," white-centric queer narratives. Executives treat diversity as a zero-sum game — ignoring the growing financial and cultural power of diverse, multicultural younger audiences.
