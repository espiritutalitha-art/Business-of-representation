Module 8.2

Modeling Your Work-in-Progress Draft

Using Grace and Frankie to show the shape of a strong 8.2 draft — before you build your own.

Goal: not a finished analysis, but a model of structure, evidence, and tension you can apply to your own case study.
0:30–1:15
Section 1 of 4

Introduction & Objectives

Name your case study. State your analytical angle. Preview the central tension. That's the whole job.

"This case study examines Grace and Frankie, Netflix's longest-running original series, as a lens into how streaming platforms represent older women — and what that representation reveals about the business calculations behind so-called 'legacy audiences.' Does its success prove aging stories are commercially viable, or reveal the limits of how far the industry will go?"
Don't overbuild this section — three moves, done fast.
1:15–2:45
Section 2 of 4

Business Analysis

Bring real data: subscribers, renewals, marketing strategy, comparable shows. Then ask what the numbers actually prove.

"Grace and Frankie ran seven seasons, becoming one of Netflix's most consistent performers without younger-skewing viral buzz. Its longevity suggests real value in reaching an older audience — but was its greenlight a genuine bet, or a low-risk show Netflix could quietly keep running?"
A strong business analysis interrogates the numbers, it doesn't just report them.
2:45–4:15
Section 3 of 4

Representation Analysis

This is usually your richest section — and where tension with the business analysis should surface.

"Grace and Frankie are funny, sexually active, entrepreneurial, and central to the plot — a challenge to TV's history of sidelining older women. But who gets this kind of representation? Both leads are white, financially secure, and conventionally attractive. The show expands representation of aging, but not of who gets to age visibly on screen."
Don't just describe representation — interrogate its edges: who's included, and who still isn't.
4:15–5:15
Section 4 of 4

Findings, Recommendations & Conclusion

Bring it together. Look forward. Make a claim about what the industry should do next.

"Grace and Frankie shows stories centering older women can be commercially sustainable, not just critically praised — but its narrow representation of which older women get this treatment reveals real limits. Recommendation: platforms betting on 'legacy audiences' should diversify who represents that audience, not just how many episodes they greenlight."
A summary restates. A conclusion recommends.

Look Ahead

Preview: What Your Peers Will Evaluate

Knowing the Peer Feedback Form now helps you draft smarter today. Reviewers will assess:

  • The Core Argument — can they restate your thesis in their own words?
  • Evidence & Support — where is your evidence strongest, and where is it thin?
  • The Tension — how well did you navigate business goals vs. representation considerations?
Ask yourself as you draft: could a classmate summarize my core claim after watching this once? That's your north star for Friday.
