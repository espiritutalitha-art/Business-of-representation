Reading 1 · Olkin / APA

Three models of disability

⚖️
Moral
Disability as flaw, punishment, or villainous marker
Example: the scarred or visibly impaired villain trope
🏥
Medical
Disability as deficit located in the individual
Examples: The Theory of Everything, Wonder
🌍
Social
Disability as produced by inaccessible systems and environments
Example: Crip Camp
Olkin gives you the conceptual engine for the entire module. The key move is not just memorizing the three models, but learning to identify which model is operating beneath a casting choice, a character arc, or a narrative resolution.

Reading 2 · Saba / UCP

Authentic casting matters

Two connected arguments
Equity: disabled actors deserve access to roles, careers, and creative influence.
Craft: authentic casting improves performance detail, storytelling quality, and audience trust.
Disabled roles cast authentically
21%
A stark gap in opportunity and representation
This reading helps students connect disability advocacy to media practice. It gives them language for arguing that authentic casting is not only ethically necessary, but also central to stronger, more believable storytelling.

Reading 4 · Geena Davis Institute

Three creator questions

1️⃣
Are disabled characters multidimensional?
Do they have goals, humor, flaws, and relationships beyond disability?
2️⃣
Do language and framing reinforce stereotypes?
Look for tragedy, pity, spectacle, or inspiration tropes.
3️⃣
Does the portrayal reflect diverse disabled experiences?
Consider race, age, class, sexuality, and disability type.
These questions take the research and turn it into practice. They help students move from broad statements about representation to close analysis of how a character or story is actually constructed on screen.

Assignments

How to use these ideas

Discussion Board
Apply one model to GDI data
Use a disability studies model to explain what the numbers reveal about the industry that raw data alone cannot.
Written Analysis
Analyze one media artifact
Use one model, Media Reader data, and all three GDI questions to build one coherent argument.
Possible examples
Crip Camp, Speechless, Wonder, Atypical, CODA, or children/YA-centered media such as Julia on Sesame Street.
Students should choose an artifact they can analyze specifically and concretely. The strongest papers will focus on scenes, character construction, and production choices rather than broad summary.
